Uninvited Guests: Early Warning Signs of Pest Infestation in Mountain Homes

By Brayson · Summit Stewards · February 3, 2025 · 5 min read

A Summit County vacation home that sits empty for weeks or months at a time is an open invitation for wildlife. Mice can squeeze through a hole the size of a dime. Squirrels and chipmunks exploit any gap in roofline trim or soffits. Carpenter ants establish colonies inside wooden structural members. And with no one home to notice the signs, small infestations become major problems. Here's how to catch them early.

Mouse and Rodent Indicators

Rodent droppings are the most obvious sign — small, dark, pellet-shaped deposits found along walls, in cabinets, behind appliances, and in utility spaces. Look also for gnaw marks on wood, drywall, food packaging, and wiring. Chewed wiring is a significant fire hazard and one of the most expensive consequences of rodent infestation. Nesting material — shredded insulation, fabric, or paper — tucked into corners, under appliances, or inside wall cavities confirms active habitation. Even a faint ammonia smell in a closed-up room suggests rodent activity.

Squirrel and Chipmunk Entry Points

Larger rodents like squirrels gain access almost exclusively through the roofline — gaps in soffits, missing or loose fascia boards, and construction gaps where roof meets wall. Signs of squirrel activity include: noises in the attic (often described as rolling or thumping sounds, especially at dawn and dusk), insulation disturbed in the attic, and nesting material concentrated near roof-level access points. Squirrels also cause serious damage by gnawing on structural members and, critically, on wiring.

Carpenter Ant Evidence

Unlike termites, carpenter ants don't eat wood — they excavate it to build galleries for their colony. The telltale sign is frass: a mix of fine wood shavings and ant body parts that looks like sawdust, found below structural wood members, windowsills, or door frames. Carpenter ants prefer wet or damaged wood, so their presence often signals a moisture problem as well. Check any area with past water damage, poorly sealed window frames, and wood that contacts soil. The structural damage from an established carpenter ant colony can be severe.

Woodpecker Damage as an Indirect Signal

Rows of small, evenly-spaced holes in the siding or trim of your home made by woodpeckers are actually a pest infestation indicator — woodpeckers drill into wood to extract insects. Their activity suggests you have wood-boring insects (larvae, beetles, or carpenter bees) inside your exterior wood. Beyond the pest problem, the holes themselves become water entry points and should be sealed promptly.

Exterior Sealing: The Best Prevention

Walk the entire perimeter of your home at ground level looking for any gap wider than 1/4 inch — around utility penetrations (gas lines, hose bibs, electrical conduit), foundation cracks, where siding meets the foundation, and around door and window frames. Every gap is a potential entry point. Steel wool, copper mesh, and hardware cloth are effective rodent-proof fillers for gaps before caulking or patching. Standard caulk alone will not deter determined rodents — they gnaw right through it.
