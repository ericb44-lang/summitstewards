7 Roof Warning Signs Every Mountain Homeowner Must Know

By Brayson · Rock Canyon Property Management · October 14, 2024 · 6 min read

Your roof is the single most critical component protecting your Summit County home. Heavy snowfall, ice dams, intense UV at altitude, and relentless freeze-thaw cycles make mountain roofs work harder than almost anywhere in the country. The problem? Most damage is invisible from the ground — until it isn't.

By the time you notice a water stain on your ceiling, the damage has likely been progressing for months. Here are the seven warning signs every mountain homeowner should know.

1. Granule Loss in Your Gutters

Asphalt shingles are coated with protective granules. When you see dark, sand-like material accumulating in your gutters or at downspout discharge points, your shingles are losing their UV protection and becoming brittle. This is especially accelerated at Summit County's elevation where UV radiation is significantly stronger. Significant granule loss typically signals shingles are in their last few years of life.

2. Ice Dams Along the Eaves

Ice dams form when heat escapes through your roof, melting snow that then refreezes at the cold eaves. The resulting ridge of ice can force water underneath your shingles and into your home. Look for large icicle formations along the roofline in winter — beautiful to look at, devastating to your roof deck and insulation. The root cause is almost always insufficient attic insulation or ventilation.

3. Damaged, Missing, or Curling Flashing

Flashing is the metal material that seals transitions — around chimneys, skylights, vents, and where the roof meets walls. When flashing lifts, cracks, or pulls away from its caulking, you have a direct water entry point. This is one of the most common sources of roof leaks and one of the most overlooked during casual inspection. Always inspect flashing joints closely, especially after a hard winter.

4. Sagging or Uneven Roof Lines

Stand back from your home and look along the roofline. It should be arrow-straight. Any dips, waves, or sags indicate structural issues — potentially compromised rafters, damaged sheathing, or even foundation movement. In Summit County, this can also result from cumulative snow load stress on an aging structure. This is never a cosmetic issue; it requires immediate professional evaluation.

5. Discolored or Dark Streaking on Shingles

Dark streaks running vertically down your roof are typically algae growth — common in shaded areas where moisture lingers. While algae itself doesn't immediately damage shingles, it retains moisture and accelerates deterioration. Greenish patches may indicate moss, which is worse — moss roots physically lift and separate shingles, allowing water infiltration. Both should be treated promptly.

6. Daylight Visible in the Attic

Get into your attic on a bright day and turn off the lights. Any pinpoints of light coming through the roof deck are entry points — for water, cold air, and pests. While you're up there, check for water staining on the rafters, wet or compressed insulation, and any soft spots in the decking. These are all signs of active or historic leaks that may not yet have shown up on your ceiling below.

7. Shingles That Are Cracked, Buckled, or Missing

After any significant wind event or heavy snowfall, do a visual scan of your roof from the ground or driveway. Missing shingles are obvious — but cracked and buckled shingles are equally serious. Buckling often means the decking beneath has warped from moisture, and cracked shingles have lost their waterproofing integrity entirely. Any missing or clearly damaged shingles should be addressed before the next precipitation event.

💡 Steward's Tip

Summit County homeowners should schedule roof inspections twice a year — once in late spring to assess winter damage, and once in early fall to ensure the roof is sealed and ready for another hard season. Don't wait for a leak to tell you there's a problem.
