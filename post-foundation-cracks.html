Not All Cracks Are Equal: How to Read Your Foundation Before It Fails

By Brayson · Rock Canyon Property Management · January 9, 2025 · 6 min read

Summit County's soil conditions and climate create one of the most challenging environments for home foundations in the Mountain West. Expansive clay soils shift with moisture changes. Hard freezes penetrate deep into the ground. Snowmelt saturates the soil around foundations every spring. The result is that virtually every home in the area has foundation cracks — but most homeowners don't know which ones to worry about.

Here is how to read what your foundation is telling you.

Hairline Cracks: Usually Normal, But Watch Them

Hairline cracks less than 1/16th of an inch wide in poured concrete foundations are extremely common and typically the result of normal concrete curing shrinkage — not structural movement. These are generally cosmetic. However, "watch them" is not idle advice: photograph them, mark the ends with a pencil, and monitor them seasonally. A hairline crack that grows wider or longer over 6-12 months is no longer just a curing crack. It's moving.

Horizontal Cracks: The Most Serious Kind

Horizontal cracks running across a concrete block or poured concrete wall are the most serious type of foundation crack you can find. They indicate lateral pressure from the soil outside — typically from hydrostatic water pressure or expansive soil pressing inward. A horizontal crack means the wall is bowing inward under pressure. Left unaddressed, this leads to wall collapse. If you find horizontal cracking in any basement or crawl space wall, contact a structural engineer before doing anything else.

Stair-Step Cracks in Block or Brick Foundations

Stair-step cracks that follow the mortar joints in concrete block or brick foundations indicate differential settlement — meaning one area of the foundation is sinking or shifting at a different rate than the adjacent area. Small stair-step cracks that remain stable may be manageable, but wide stair-step cracks (wider than 1/4 inch) or cracks where one side of the crack is offset from the other (one block higher than its neighbor) indicate significant movement that needs professional evaluation.

Vertical Cracks: Context Dependent

Vertical cracks in a poured concrete wall can be either settling or shrinkage. A vertical crack near the center of a wall that is uniform in width top-to-bottom is most likely a shrinkage crack. However, a vertical crack that is wider at the top than the bottom (or vice versa) indicates differential settlement — one side of the wall has moved more than the other. Similarly, vertical cracks in corners often indicate the foundation corner is moving independently. Width, location, and whether the crack is growing are all key factors.

Diagonal Cracks from Window and Door Corners

Cracks running diagonally from the corners of basement windows or doors are stress fractures caused by settlement. The openings create weak points in the wall, and when the foundation moves, cracks radiate from those corners under load. These are usually moderate in severity but should be monitored. If they appear in interior drywall as well — mirroring the pattern above — the movement may be significant enough to affect the structure's framing.

What to Do When You Find a Concerning Crack

The most important step is documentation. Photograph every crack with a ruler or coin in frame for scale. Note the date. Mark the ends of the crack with pencil or tape. Re-check in 90 days. Growing cracks — any crack that gets wider, longer, or displaces — warrant a structural engineer inspection, not a handyman with hydraulic cement. Patching a crack without understanding the cause is cosmetic at best and misleading at worst.

Buyers and their inspectors will want to see that any cracks have been stable for years.
