The Summit County Spring Inspection Checklist Every Homeowner Needs

By Brayson · Rock Canyon Property Management · March 20, 2025 · 8 min read

Spring in Summit County is a revelation — and a reckoning. As snow melts and temperatures rise, your home reveals everything winter did to it while you weren't watching. Ice dams leave their mark. Ground movement exposes itself in new cracks. Frozen pipes that thawed may be dripping behind walls. Spring is the single most important time of year to do a thorough home inspection, and doing it systematically makes all the difference.

Here is the room-by-room, system-by-system checklist we walk through on every Rock Canyon Property Management spring inspection.

Exterior: Start Outside

Roof and gutters: Walk the perimeter and visually scan the roof from ground level. Look for missing or damaged shingles, lifted flashing, and any areas of visible sagging. Clear gutters of debris accumulated under the snow. Check downspouts are firmly attached and directing water away from the foundation — at least 6 feet out.

Foundation perimeter: Walk the entire foundation line looking for new cracks, heaved sections, or any gaps where the foundation meets the sill plate. Note the grade — after snowmelt, soil often settles and may now slope toward the foundation instead of away from it. Negative grade is a leading cause of basement water intrusion.

Driveway and hardscape: Frost heave is brutal on paved surfaces. Note any new cracks, heaved sections, or areas where pavement has separated from the structure. Retaining walls should be checked for any new lean or displacement — these can fail suddenly when saturated spring soil adds weight and pressure.

Wood surfaces: Inspect all painted or stained wood — decks, fences, trim, railings, siding — for peeling, cracking, or soft spots that indicate moisture infiltration. Press a screwdriver into any suspicious soft area. If it penetrates easily, the wood has rotted and needs replacement. Spring is the ideal time to restain or reseal wood before summer UV exposure.

Roof and Attic

Get into the attic as soon as temperatures allow. Look for: water staining on rafters and sheathing from ice dams; compressed, wet, or displaced insulation; any daylight visible through the roof deck; and signs of animal activity. Check that all attic vents are clear and unobstructed — blocked ventilation caused by snow and ice often leads to moisture buildup that can persist into summer.

Plumbing: Check Every Fixture

Even if you winterized your plumbing, spring is the time to verify every fixture. Turn on every faucet and check under every sink for drips or signs of past leaking. Flush every toilet and observe the fill cycle. Check the water heater for rust, corrosion, or moisture at connections. If you have a boiler system, bleed radiators and check system pressure. Inspect the pressure relief valve on your water heater — it should open and close cleanly without dripping.

If your home has an irrigation system, have it inspected before activation. Heads can crack over winter, manifolds can shift, and a system activated with a broken head wastes hundreds of gallons and can create soil saturation against your foundation.

HVAC: Transition to Cooling Season

Replace furnace filters — they've been working hard all winter. If you have a heat pump, clean the outdoor coil of any debris, leaves, or matted grass. Test the air conditioning or cooling mode of your system before you need it in summer — discovering a non-functional AC in July is a much worse experience than in April. Check that all supply and return registers are open and unobstructed. Inspect ductwork in the attic or crawl space for any disconnections or damage from rodent activity over winter.

Interior: Walls, Floors, and Windows

Walk every room looking specifically for: new cracks in drywall (especially diagonal cracks at corners of doors and windows); any staining on ceilings or walls; and soft or spongy spots in flooring. Open every window and door and test their operation — if a door or window that previously operated freely now sticks or binds, the frame may have shifted, which can indicate foundation or structural movement.

Check caulking around all tubs, showers, and windows. Winter humidity changes and temperature extremes crack and shrink caulk. Any gap in caulking at wet areas is a water entry point.

Garage and Outbuildings

Test the garage door auto-reverse safety feature. Inspect the garage door weatherstripping — it takes a beating from snowplows and shoveling and frequently tears or dislodges. Check the ceiling of the garage for water staining from the roof above. If you have a detached shed or outbuilding, inspect the roof, check for rodent entry points, and assess the foundation for any movement.

Document Everything

The single most valuable thing you can do during a spring inspection — beyond finding issues — is documenting what you find. Photographs with dates create a historical record that is invaluable for tracking whether a crack is growing, whether a water stain is new, and whether a repair has held. That documentation also becomes a significant asset when you eventually sell the property.
